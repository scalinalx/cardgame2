# PASS 1 — TM, expansions, and general euro-excitement principles

**Stats:** 5 angles · 22 sources fetched · 86 claims extracted · 25 verified · **20 confirmed, 5 killed** · 104 agent calls.

**Top-line summary (verbatim):**
> Top thematic euros generate "exciting/tense/play-again" feelings primarily through **player
> agency** (multiple genuinely good options forcing trade-offs), a satisfying **exponential growth
> arc** (weak-to-powerful), **direct interaction and adaptive tension** (shared events, jockeying
> for position), and **multi-turn planning hooks** (e.g. Ark Nova's card-strength escalator) — none
> of which inherently require mental arithmetic. The "too much math" problem in Terraforming Mars is
> a SEPARATE, fixable layer: it comes from forcing players to hold many tracked resources, passive
> card effects, and per-card conversion costs in working memory. The fix is **cognitive offloading**
> — externalize game state into persistent visible components (tracks, tokens, player-board layouts)
> and replace spendable/convertible resources with **flat standing discounts** and clean
> iconography, as Ares Expedition did by turning steel/titanium into permanent discounts. Crucially,
> **excitement and arithmetic are decoupled**: TM's beloved core (its two-level engine of production
> + one-off card effects) is praised independent of its disliked bookkeeping. The strongest single
> lever for a 2-player TM-like is adding low-load, all-affecting tension and interaction since TM's
> most common complaints — solitaire feel, runaway leader, endgame slog, poor end-condition scaling
> at 2 players — are exactly what Turmoil-style interaction and proper end-trigger tuning address.

## Pass 1 — CONFIRMED findings (verbatim claim + evidence)

### 1. Player agency / meaningful trade-offs — high-fun, low-load. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Excitement in deep euros is driven primarily by player agency and meaningful trade-offs:
the best games create tension by presenting two or more genuinely good options to choose between.
Adds NO arithmetic or rules overhead.
**Evidence:** "The feeling of agency (control over what happens) is one of the strongest drivers of
engagement. The best games create tension between two or more good options." Corroborated by Sid
Meier's canonical "a game is a series of interesting decisions" (GDC 2012) and design literature
requiring "no single option clearly better than the others." The difficulty is in *choosing*, not
*computing*.
**Source:** streamlinedgaming.com/what-makes-a-board-game-fun/

### 2. The growth-arc is the core hook ("numbers go up"). (3-0, high)
**Claim:** The core emotional appeal of engine-builders is the growth arc — going from few
resources/limited options to feeling powerful by game's end, producing impressive, surprising
outcomes. Players feel acceleration, not velocity, so a snowballing tableau that "finally clicks" is
the central hook.
**Evidence:** players "begin with few resources and limited options and throughout the game grow
their acquisitions exponentially such that by the end of the game they feel powerful relative to
their starting state... can result in impressive and surprising outcomes." Echoed by reviewers
comparing TM to Cookie Clicker ("watching your numbers go up") and Wingspan ("snowball your power and
dominate later"). **The payoff feeling is what designers should protect and amplify.**
**Source:** boardgamesnob.com/2017/07/29/the-problem-of-engine-building-games-part-1/

### 3. Exponential growth is NOT automatically fun — it creates the runaway-leader problem. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Exponential engine growth inherently creates a design problem (runaway-leader /
rich-get-richer) that must be ACTIVELY mitigated — a TM-like must deliberately build in catch-up,
diminishing returns, multiple win conditions, or interaction rather than assuming the engine alone
carries excitement.
**Evidence:** "exponential games inherently pose a challenge to game designers that they must address
using at least one of the solutions." Corroborated by positive-feedback-loop theory (Machinations.io,
BGDF): diminishing returns, storage caps, multiple win conditions, interaction, catch-up loops.
**Nuance:** some euro designers argue mild runaway leaders are acceptable in soft-competition games,
so over-correcting toward forced-equal scores is itself a risk.
**Source:** boardgamesnob.com/2017/07/29/the-problem-of-engine-building-games-part-1/

### 4. Multi-turn planning hooks — Ark Nova's action "escalator". (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Multi-turn planning hooks create engagement by letting players set up satisfying future
payoffs. Ark Nova's action-card-row "escalator" (borrowed from Civilization: A New Dawn) — unused
actions grow stronger over time so players strategically delay powerful actions to maximize impact —
is repeatedly credited as a source of engagement. The tension is in *sequencing*, not computation.
**Evidence:** "you might set up a killer follow-up turn by moving a card to the top of your strength
track to execute something serious." Wikipedia: "unused actions become more powerful over time...
allows players to strategically delay powerful actions to maximize their impact later." Coopboardgames:
"gives you scope for planning, as well as some interesting decisions." Adds anticipation/planning depth
without resource math.
**Source:** meeplemountain.com/reviews/ark-nova-first-take/

### 5. Reduce load by EXTERNALIZING state into visible components. (3-0, high) — strongest-sourced
**Claim:** Cognitive load is reduced — without removing depth — by externalizing game state into
persistent, visible components (scoring tracks, position markers, player-board layouts) so players
don't hold it in working memory. The foundational "no added load" technique.
**Evidence:** NN/g (Nielsen Norman Group, field-defining UX authority): "Board games use various
techniques to convey this information and reduce cognitive load... scoring tracks to communicate
player progress... pawns to mark positioning," and "as board-game design has grown more complex,
players' working memory has become a crucial resource to manage." Corroborated by peer-reviewed
cognitive-offloading research: physical action that alters information-processing requirements confers
a performance advantage, especially at higher memory loads. Recognition-over-recall: standardizing/
limiting visible options lowers load.
**Source:** nngroup.com/articles/usability-heuristics-board-games/ (PRIMARY-quality)

### 6. Iconography is double-edged — TM is the cautionary tale. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Clean, learnable icons offload rules and reduce load, but heavy or unintuitive iconography
BACKFIRES, increasing load by forcing rulebook lookups. TM is specifically cited as a real example of
overwhelming iconography requiring lookups.
**Evidence:** NN/g: "these icons (which can be tricky to understand) may only increase the cognitive
load on players," with a Minecraft: Builders and Biomes example of "pausing the game and referencing
the rulebook." Multiple design sources (Mindclash Games, League of Gamemakers, Board Game Design
Course) cite TM specifically as having overwhelming iconography requiring repeated reference-card/
rulebook lookups.
**Source:** nngroup.com/articles/usability-heuristics-board-games/

### 7. TM's most-praised strength is its two-level engine — separate from the math. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** TM's most-praised strength is its two-level engine — a production engine plus one-off/blue
action-and-effect cards — whose interplay and synergy make each game different. This excitement source
is mechanically SEPARATE from its disliked arithmetic, proving you can keep/amplify the engine payoff
while cutting the math.
**Evidence:** "one of the best engines I've experienced in a board game... The interplay and synergy
between these two levels make each play of Terraforming Mars different." Corroborated by rolltoreview
("The interplay between cards and the engines you can build is unmatched") and thethoughtfulgamer.
Critics' complaints target length/math/interaction, NOT the engine — confirming fun and load are
decouplable.
**Source:** meeplemountain.com/reviews/terraforming-mars/

### 8. TM imposes heavy bookkeeping/working-memory load — adds work, NOT fun. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Each player tracks at least six resources on their player board (MegaCredits, Steel,
Titanium, Plants, Energy, Heat), plus Terraform Rating on the main board, plus all passive card
effects, plus a flat 3-credit cost per card kept. First-time players perceive "a lot of math"; at
least one reported feeling "dumb" and doubting their addition.
**Evidence:** Thoughtful Gamer: "Each person is tracking no fewer than six different resources on
their player board, plus one that laps around the main board, plus all of the passive effects."
First-impressions player: "There is a lot of math in this game... could be a turn off to some"; "I
felt really dumb playing the game whenever it was my turn, and the constant doubt that I wasn't adding
something up correctly." This is bookkeeping/computation, distinct from the engine's fun.
**Sources:** thethoughtfulgamer.com/2020/04/21/...; lakeshoregamenight.wordpress.com/2018/08/24/...

### 9. THE fix: replace spendable resources with flat standing discounts (Ares Expedition). (3-0, high)
**Claim:** The best fix for the arithmetic problem is to replace spendable/convertible resources with
flat standing discounts (smart costing) and to offload tracking. Ares Expedition turns TM's spendable
steel/titanium into continuous, permanent discounts on building/space-tag cards rather than stockpiled
resources you convert per-card — removing a whole layer of resource tracking and per-card conversion
math while keeping the strategic tag-synergy depth. REDUCES load without reducing depth.
**Evidence:** steel and titanium "serve as continuous discounts on certain projects, rather than being
spendable resources." Official Dized rules: "permanent capabilities" (−2 MC per steel on building
tags, −3 MC per titanium on space tags) that "do not produce any resources" and are not consumed when
used. A flat standing discount replaces repeated arithmetic.
**Source:** meeplemountain.com/reviews/terraforming-mars-ares-expedition/
> **(This is the model for our open issue A1 — Wood/Crystal → passive tag discounts.)**

### 10. Simultaneous/hidden phase-selection reduces downtime (Ares Expedition). (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Ares replaces TM's turn-by-turn play with five phases (Development, Construction, Action,
Production, Research) chosen secretly and resolved together, cutting playtime from ~3-4 hrs to ~1 hr
with near-zero downtime. (But can backfire at 2p — see #11.)
**Evidence:** hidden simultaneous selection then "reveal the cards. Activate the phases chosen."
Dized: "To speed up the game, players should resolve each phase simultaneously." Akin to Race for the
Galaxy / Puerto Rico role selection.
**Source:** meeplemountain.com/reviews/terraforming-mars-ares-expedition/

### 11. Streamlining structure ALONE doesn't cure arithmetic — can worsen it at 2p. (3-0, medium)
**Claim:** In Ares' 2-player mode, fewer active phases per round means MORE rounds, MORE cards, MORE
variables to track — one reviewer "dreaded the Production and Action phases" and called it a "grind."
Lesson: attack the math at the resource/iconography level, not just via faster turn structure.
**Evidence:** (2P section) "Having fewer active phases per round means you'll have more rounds...
pushing you to mentally track more variables. As a result, I dreaded the Production and Action phases,
since I had to channel my inner back alley accountant... none of them wanted to go through the grind a
second time." Single attributed reviewer (others more positive on Ares overall) → medium confidence,
mechanically coherent.
**Source:** meeplemountain.com/reviews/terraforming-mars-ares-expedition/

### 12. TM's endgame is a slog/bookkeeping — design a crisp dramatic ending. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** TM's endgame is widely felt as a slog — "ticking marks off a checklist," squeezing out
final points and just advancing trackers. A TM-like should design a crisp, dramatic endgame (tightening
triggers, late-game scoring tension, a climactic event) rather than letting the engine idle. Pacing/
drama fix, not arithmetic.
**Evidence:** Thoughtful Gamer: "The end of every game... has been a bit of a slog... trying to squeeze
out additional points... It's like ticking marks off a checklist." Meeple Mountain: in 2P "you're just
advancing the trackers for the game and scoring points."
**Sources:** thethoughtfulgamer.com/...; meeplemountain.com/reviews/terraforming-mars/

### 13. TM's end conditions DON'T scale with player count — acute for a 2p game. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** At 2 players the engine runs 2-3 turns too long (the part that drags); at 5 players there
isn't time to fully run the engine. For a 2-PLAYER TM-like specifically, tuning the end trigger so the
game stops while the engine is still satisfying-but-not-exhausted is high-impact, zero-extra-load.
**Evidence:** "the end conditions don't scale"; 2P "your engine runs for 2-3 turns too long"; 5P "you
don't really have enough time to run your engine fully." Mechanically grounded: TM's global parameters
(14% O2, 9 oceans, +8C) are fixed regardless of player count, so generation count varies widely
(BGG: 12-14 generations at 2-3p vs 5-10 at 4-5p).
**Source:** meeplemountain.com/reviews/terraforming-mars/

### 14. TM has a runaway-leader / rich-get-richer dynamic — acute at 2p. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Income = production + Terraform Rating, so a leader compounds, making catch-up hard,
especially vs. experienced players. In a 2-player TM-like this is acute (no third party to slow the
leader), so explicit catch-up or interaction levers matter to keep both players feeling they can win.
**Evidence:** Meeple Mountain: "The 'rich get richer' phenomenon will mean... It's easy enough to get
left in the dust to a more experienced player" (more terraforming → more income → more cards). TR
compounds income each generation. **Caveat:** some players enjoy playing from behind; avoid
over-correcting.
**Sources:** meeplemountain.com/reviews/terraforming-mars/; boardgamesnob.com/...part-1/

### 15. Direct all-affecting interaction is the strongest "solitaire-fix" — TM Turmoil model. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Adding direct, all-affecting interaction and adaptive tension is the strongest lever to fix
the "solitaire-y, not exciting" problem — and it can be done at LOW arithmetic cost. TM's Turmoil
expansion adds a political board (Terraforming Committee) players jockey over plus Global Events
affecting all players, credited with fostering direct competition, more opportunities to disrupt
opponents, and a new level of tension/adaptability (forcing players to plan for global consequences).
**Evidence:** Turmoil "fosters more direct competition as players vie for political power... more
opportunities to disrupt opponents' plans," contrasting the base game that "can feel more solitary."
Global Events "introduce unexpected challenges and opportunities that can affect all players... a new
level of tension and adaptability." Corroborated by Gamereactor, RPGnet, boardgamecafe ("seeing a
distant event turned over that will eliminate hard-earned resources is a shock").
**Source:** frontlinegaming.org/2024/09/11/...turmoil-review/

### 16. BUT the interaction fix must be LOW-load — Turmoil is a cautionary tale. (3-0, high)
**Claim:** Turmoil "significantly ramps up complexity," adds phases/decisions that lengthen turns and
raise analysis-paralysis and downtime risk — though foreknowledge (Global Events visible ~3 generations
ahead) partially offsets AP, and many players judge the added tension worthwhile. **Takeaway: borrow the
all-affecting-shared-event and contested-position TENSION, but implement it with minimal extra rules/
phases and built-in foreknowledge to pre-empt AP.**
**Evidence:** Turmoil "significantly ramps up the complexity," adds "additional phases and decisions,
which can drag out an already lengthy game," with "higher risk of analysis paralysis... longer turns
and more downtime." Officially an "Expert Expansion." Mitigation: Global Events revealed 3 generations
in advance. Many reviewers still call the trade worthwhile ("made the already very fun game even
better").
**Sources:** frontlinegaming.org/...; boardgamegeek.com/boardgame/273473/...; zatu.com/...

# THE NOCLIP HOMM3 TRANSCRIPT — key design lessons (parsed)

Source: noclip Hall-of-Fame pitch video transcript (enthusiast appreciation of HOMM3, with John Van
Caneghem clips). Used for *what feeling to chase*, not balance numbers.

1. **"It's like a board game but WAY BETTER than any board game I've ever played."** The whole framing:
   HOMM3 as a board game that transcends the form — via **theme, art, and a living world**, not deeper
   math.
2. **"One more turn."** Named explicitly alongside Civ. The hook is **momentum & anticipation** — wanting
   to see the next thing unfold (matches the growth-arc "click").
3. **The MAP is the excitement.** ~80% of the playthrough is the adventure map: racing rivals to
   resources, Pandora's Boxes, **obelisks revealing a buried Grail**, two-way teleporters → "they can
   sneak in your back door." **Shared-space racing = the #1 low-load excitement lever AND the most HOMM3
   thing.**
4. **"Astrologers proclaim the Week of the Squirrel — all dwellings increase population."** Called out by
   name — our proclamation system is beloved HOMM3 flavour. (Research says make it interactive; HOMM3
   shows the tone to keep.)
5. **Tiny, evocative, hand-made art; "intimate screens"** (the mage-tower view, curved earth) — "the most
   talented 15-year-old of the 90s." Atmosphere over systems.
6. **Decisive-victory tension & retreat** — combat where you weigh "go in overwhelming vs. leave them a
   chance to flee." A player-CHOSEN risk that makes stories, not arithmetic (the push-your-luck lever).
7. **JVC's design wisdom:** the "magic" came together in the LAST 3 WEEKS via pacing/turn-length/
   move-distance tweaks; QA hated it until suddenly they were "arguing over which strategy is best to
   win — that's my moment of clarity." → Lesson: **fun emerges from PACING/feel iteration, not from
   adding content.**

**Convergence:** transcript + both research passes AGREE — HOMM3's magic = theme + a contested shared
map + momentum/anticipation + atmosphere, **none of it arithmetic.** Biggest opportunity for our game:
turn the hex map from solitaire tile-placement into a HOMM3-style **contested adventure**.
